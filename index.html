Computational Linguistic Analysis of Online Media

Background

This project is an analysis of news media using Python's industry-standard natural language programming libraries, including NLTK, Textacy, Scikit-Learn, MALLET, and others. By leveraging NLP paradigms, this project demonstrates the value of core computational linguistic concepts: scraping datasets from the web; pre-processing text as part of text sanitization; and examining corpora quantitatively through TF-IDF, PCA, LDA and other methods.

Project focus

In the 21st century, the threat to and importance of journalism have never been greater. As such, the goal of this project is to study the language of various news outlets using natural language processing methods to determine if there are any discernible patterns as to how news is reported and what distinguishes sites from one another.

Define database

The first step in approaching the analysis is to determine the news outlets to study. The sites chosenBreitbart, CNN, Economist, Fox, Mother Jones, New York Times, Vox, Wall Street Journal, Yahoowere based on two criteria: popularity and partisan leaning.

Popularity was gauged by online traffic. Two thirds of the media outlets chosen amass more than 100 million page views in one month, qualifying them to be among the most 100 most read U.S. media publications, according to data analytics firm SimilarWeb. Sanitize Dataset

Sanitizing the data is a crucial step to ensure that the text data is in a suitable format for analysis. The following preprocessing steps were taken: tokenization, punctuation removal, stopwords removal, names of people removal, case unification, lemmatization.

  • Tokenization: Involves breaking the text into individual words or tokens. This step is essential because most NLP tasks operate on a word-by-word basis, and tokenization provides the basic units for analysis.
  • Remove Punctuation: Punctuation marks often do not carry significant meaning in many NLP tasks. Removing punctuation helps reduce noise in the data and makes it easier to analyze the text.
  • Remove Stopwords: Stopwords are common words that occur frequently in a language but often do not carry much semantic meaning (e.g., "the", "is", "and"). Removing stopwords helps reduce the dimensionality of the data and focuses analysis on words that are more informative.
  • Remove Names of People: In some NLP tasks, such as sentiment analysis or topic modeling, the presence of specific named entities like people's names may not be relevant and could introduce bias or noise. Removing names of people helps generalize the text and focus on the underlying themes or sentiments.
  • Unify Case: Standardizing the case of words (e.g., converting all words to lowercase) helps ensure consistency in the data and avoids treating the same word differently based on its capitalization. For example, "Apple" and "apple" should be considered the same word in many NLP tasks.
  • Lemmatize: Lemmatization involves reducing words to their base or canonical form (i.e., their lemma). This step helps normalize different forms of words to their common root, which reduces the complexity of the data and improves the accuracy of analysis. For example, "running" and "ran" would both be lemmatized to "run".

By performing these preprocessing steps, the text data is transformed into a cleaner, more standardized format that is better suited for various NLP tasks such as text classification, sentiment analysis, named entity recognition, and more.

Whether, over time, outlets have modified their content to cater to their readers bite-sized preferences and byte-sized, on-the-go readership habits would be an interesting area of further research.

def get_random_slice(corpus, parm_slice_length):
    last_possible_starting_position = len(corpus) - parm_slice_length - 1
    starting_position = random.randint(0, last_possible_starting_position)
    ending_position = starting_position + parm_slice_length
    token_slice = corpus[starting_position: ending_position]
    return token_slice
